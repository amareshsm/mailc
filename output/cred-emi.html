you see "no-cost EMI" on a ₹50,000 laptop.
it sounds like the store is doing you a favor.
but when you check later, you paid ₹5,000 more
than the cash price, and your credit card limit is
now ₹50,000 shorter.

LET'S GET INTO

the fine print

when a retailer offers "no-cost EMI" or "0% EMI,"
it means you pay in monthly installments.
but it's not interest-free.

on many no-cost EMI plans, the bank still charges
interest as per the regular EMI schedule.
what makes it feel "no-cost" is that this interest
is passed to you as an upfront discount.

so your statement may still show EMI interest,
while that upfront discount offsets it.
processing fee, GST on interest, and foreclosure
charges can still apply.

what happens to
your credit limit?

the moment you confirm an EMI purchase, the full
amount is counted against your credit limit,
immediately. so if your credit limit is ₹1,00,000 and
you buy a ₹50,000 laptop on EMI, you don't
have ₹95,000 to use next week.

you have ₹50,000. the rest comes back gradually,
as you pay each installment.
